Building a Service Design-Led Market Entry Strategy for Exeen

A service design-led approach to launching a consumer brand in one of India's most competitive categories.

Overview

Exeen approached Imai while operating as a B2B supplier of essential oils. The company wanted to enter the consumer market and viewed a surface cleaner as its first step toward building a larger FMCG brand.

The ambition was clear: create a consumer brand capable of achieving mass-market adoption.

The challenge was equally clear.

The household cleaning category was already dominated by established players with deep distribution networks, significant advertising budgets, and strong consumer recall. Competing head-on with brands like Lizol was unrealistic for a new entrant operating on a limited launch budget of approximately ₹5 lakhs.

Rather than designing another cleaner brand, we set out to design a market-entry system that could help a challenger brand earn trust, trial, and adoption with limited resources.

“The goal wasn't launching a cleaner. It was creating a system that could earn trust, trial, and advocacy with almost no room for error.”

The Challenge

The product already existed.

It was available in multiple fragrances and had the backing of the client's manufacturing capabilities. However, it lacked meaningful differentiation, positioning, and a clear route to market.

Our research revealed three critical constraints:

  • Limited launch budget
  • No large-scale distribution network
  • Competition from established national brands

Traditional FMCG launch strategies rely on scale. Exeen could not afford scale.

The question became:

How can a new brand achieve meaningful market penetration without competing on advertising spend?

Research & Discovery

We began by studying:

  • Existing cleaning brands
  • Consumer cleaning habits
  • Distribution models
  • FMCG launch strategies
  • Market-entry case studies

As we explored different possibilities, one insight became increasingly clear:

Trying to reach everyone would result in reaching no one. A statewide or nationwide launch would spread resources too thin across distribution, awareness, and activation. Instead of maximizing reach, we needed to maximize concentration.

What research told us?

While competing with industry giants with huge market cap and budget is futile, concentrating efforts and money to a selected region could stand a chance there and then spread outwards. [Image generated using AI]

The Strategic Shift

We reframed the challenge.

Instead of asking:

"How do we launch across India?"

We asked:

"How do we become impossible to ignore in one place?"

This led to a micro-market penetration strategy. The objective was simple:

  1. Dominate a carefully selected local market.
  2. Build awareness through repeated exposure.
  3. Drive trial through sampling.
  4. Create word-of-mouth adoption.
  5. Expand gradually into adjacent markets.

What we later learned aligns closely with concepts such as diffusion of innovation and geographic market penetration, although at the time the framework emerged organically through research and strategic exploration.

Selecting the Launch Market

The market selection process considered:

  • Manufacturing proximity
  • Distribution feasibility
  • Logistics costs
  • Sampling efficiency
  • Word-of-mouth potential
  • Population diversity

Several districts were evaluated, including Ernakulam, Kottayam, and Idukki. Through discussions with the client, Kottayam emerged as the most viable launch market.

Key factors included:

  • Lower distribution costs
  • Easier operational management
  • Better access from the production facility
  • A more diverse and mobile population
  • Strong potential for word-of-mouth diffusion

The goal was to create depth of penetration before pursuing breadth of expansion.

Designing the Exeen Experience

Our research also revealed another challenge. Most cleaning brands focused on functional benefits such as germ protection and disinfection. Exeen needed a more emotional point of differentiation.

We developed the concept of:

The Exeen Experience

Exeen was positioned not merely as a cleaning product, but as the final step in a cleaning ritual.

A product that leaves behind more than cleanliness. A product that creates a feeling. The brand experience was built around three pillars:

Pristine Finish

Visible cleanliness and a well-maintained environment.

Safe Touch

A gentle and non-irritating experience for households.

Joyful Atmosphere

Fragrance, freshness, and emotional uplift inspired by the company's expertise in essential oils.

Rather than competing solely on utility, Exeen aimed to enhance the overall experience of cleaning.

Service Design Approach

Instead of treating marketing as isolated campaigns, we designed a journey that exposed consumers to the brand at multiple stages of decision-making.

Objective

The objective was not a single moment of exposure.

It was repeated interaction across the customer journey until trust was established.

The Rollout Model

The proposed system combined:

  • Hyperlocal distribution
  • Retail activation
  • Sampling programs
  • Field marketing
  • Community engagement
  • Local advertising
  • Digital reinforcement

The model was intentionally designed to work within the client's operational and financial constraints while creating measurable market presence.

Outcome

Initial execution included:

  • Local advertising efforts
  • Field-level activations
  • Product distribution in the target market

However, during the rollout phase, the client encountered internal operational and production challenges that required a pause in expansion activities.

As a result, the full system was not executed at scale.

Despite this, the project delivered:

  • A differentiated brand positioning
  • A market-entry strategy
  • A service design framework for adoption
  • A scalable geographic expansion model
  • A roadmap for future consumer brand growth

Reflection

This project changed how I think about branding.

The challenge was never designing a logo, a package, or a campaign.

The challenge was designing a system.

A system that connected operations, distribution, marketing, customer experience, and brand perception into a coherent path toward adoption.

What began as a branding assignment ultimately became a service design exercise in market creation, proving that for emerging brands, strategy often matters more than scale.
