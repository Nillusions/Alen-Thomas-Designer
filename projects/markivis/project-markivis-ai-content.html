An AI-Powered Content Production System for a B2B Marketing Agency

When I stepped into LinkedIn content at Markivis, the function was early-stage — no established system, no defined cadence, posts going out once a month at best. The brief was to help with content. I saw an opportunity to do something more foundational.

I built an end-to-end, human in the loop, AI content production system from scratch — a brand-trained writing agent, a node-based image generation pipeline, a video workflow, and a cast of six original comic characters. Designed it, ran it, proved it worked, then documented and handed it off to the team.

It started as something I wanted to try. It ended up becoming the content infrastructure the team now runs on.

Outcome: 200% LinkedIn impression growth, AI detection reduced across content, content production time reduced from hours to 30 minutes

The Problem

The workflow was entirely dependent on who was available and how much time they had.

A consultant would provide content ideas. I would write the copy. I would design the visuals. I would post. If anyone was unavailable the whole chain broke.

There was no content strategy, no pillar structure, no consistent visual language. Posts went up once a month at best. When they did go up they were generic — stock photo backgrounds, gradient cards promoting blog posts, holiday greetings that looked like every other B2B agency on LinkedIn.

The content had no personality, no differentiation, and no measurable impact.

The deeper problem

The real issue wasn't output quality. It was that the entire operation was manual, scattered, and unsustainable. One person doing everything — ideation, writing, design, scheduling — with other client deliverables on the same plate.

Scaling this without a system was impossible.

Key Objectives

Build a scalable content infrastructure — reduce content production time from hours to 30 minutes, establish a consistent brand voice, and create a system the team can run independently.

The Insight

Good content at scale needs a system, not just effort.

The writing could be systematised — if the AI understood the brand voice deeply enough. The visuals could be systematised — if the generation was structured and controlled. The strategy could be systematised — if there was a clear content mix tied to business goals. Also a human overseeing this can make the creative side of all of it much better and also create less errors.

All three together would turn a one-person bottleneck into a scalable production operation.

Building the System

Layer 1 — The AI Writing Agent

The first challenge was making AI write like a human. Not "less AI sounding" — actually undetectable.

I chose Mistral to build the agent. The decision was deliberate — Mistral gave me more control over agent behaviour than other tools at the time. I spent days researching, writing and rewriting the system prompt until the output consistently passed AI detection tools at 0%. [The 0% AI-detection result was observed across multiple testing platforms during development, but AI detection tools are inherently unreliable and should not be treated as definitive. The goal was to reduce editing effort by generating more natural, brand-consistent content—not to optimize for detection scores.]

The agent wasn't just a writing tool. It was structured to interview first — asking about post type, platform, any personal observations or experiences to weave in, and any specific angles. Only then would it generate the full post — hook, body, CTA, hashtags — calibrated to the platform and content type.

The system prompt was structured in XML with five layers:

  • Brand guidelines — brand tokens, what to avoid, checklist
  • Writing style — output format, tone, voice, humanisation rules, platform-specific instructions
  • Thinking style — audience knowledge, how to approach writing, personality
  • Examples — by content type
  • Comic instructions — character descriptions, narrative rules, themes

The result was a single-shot prompt that produced 90% ready content. Human review and light editing handled the remaining 10%.

Layer 2 — The Image Pipeline

Designing every visual from scratch in Figma with a full client workload wasn't sustainable. I needed a system that was fast, controlled, and brand consistent.

I explored Figma Weave (then Weavy) but ran out of credits quickly. When Freepik launched Spaces — a node-based creative generation engine — it solved the problem. Node-based generation meant control at every step. I wasn't throwing prompts into the dark and hoping. I was directing the output — brand style input, character references, composition logic, output formatting.

The pipeline connected brand style references to the generation nodes, producing images that were consistent with Markivis visual identity without manual design work for every post.

The final step — adding text, fine-tuning composition — happened in Figma using a template system I built with Figma Buzz. Multiple template variants meant the social media team could produce finished posts without starting from scratch every time.

The Comic Character Campaign

The posts were working. Impressions were climbing. But everything felt like thought leadership — the same register as every other B2B marketing agency.

I wanted something different. Something with personality that competitors wouldn't do.

The answer was a comic character universe built specifically for Markivis content.

The cast:

Max the Marketer — The protagonist. Witty, burnt-out yet endlessly creative. Always holding a tablet and a half-empty coffee cup. Vivid Purple jacket, Lime Yellow sneakers. Represents Markivis's balance of strategy and chaos.

Dot (AI Companion) — A floating glowing orb inspired by the Markivis dot icon. Changes colour based on mood — purple for thoughtful, orange for excited, lime for sarcastic. Max's AI sidekick.

Pixel the Designer — Always surrounded by sketch pads and motion blur lines. Round glasses, mismatched socks, gradient hair streaks. The embodiment of "creative with intent."

Arti the Analyst — Calm, methodical, slightly robotic but endearing. Deep Navy with Lime accents. Always holding dashboards that comically react to emotions.

Nova the Strategist — The visionary thinker. Sharp posture, geometric patterns from Markivis's arrow motif. Speech bubbles showing strategy pyramids or arrows to nowhere.

Sunny the Client — Sunrise Orange, exaggerated gestures. Says things like "make it viral" and "we'll know it when we see it." Loveable chaos energy.

The narrative logic was simple:

  • Max is the captain
  • Nova advises him on strategy
  • Arti executes campaigns
  • Pixel makes it real
  • Dot supports with AI precision
  • Sunny challenges everything like a true client

The Content Strategy

The system wasn't just tools — it had a strategic framework underneath it.

Markivis' content strategy combines thought leadership, service showcases, employee stories, educational content, videos, comics, and case studies to guide audiences through the marketing funnel. While thought leadership and culture-focused content build awareness, educational carousels and service videos foster consideration and trust. Case studies and proof-based content then help convert prospects by demonstrating real results and business impact.
Each content type had a defined tool in the pipeline — ChatGPT project for copy, Freepik Spaces for images, Claude + Remotion for video, Canva for simple graphics, Figma Buzz for templated layouts.

How the Full Workflow Runs

Content idea → Content bank → ChatGPT project

The agent asks: post type, platform, personal angle, tone.

Generates 5 hook options. Human selects one.

AI continues with full post — description, visual prompt, CTA.

For image content:

Visual prompt → Freepik Spaces → Image output → Figma Buzz template → Designer adds text → Final post

For video content:

Script → Scene descriptions → Claude Code + Remotion → Video output → Editor/Canva → Final post

For comic content:

Scenario brief → ChatGPT generates script → Freepik Spaces generates character scene → Canva for composition → Final post

Learnings

A system beats effort every time.

Manual processes don't scale. The 200% impression growth didn't come from working harder — it came from building something that could produce consistently without heroics.

Humanisation is a design problem.

Making AI write like a human required the same thinking as any other design problem — understand the user (the reader), define the constraints (what feels human vs robotic), iterate until it works. The 0% AI detection result was a design outcome, not a technical one.

Personality differentiates more than quality.

The comic character campaign consistently outperformed straight thought leadership posts. B2B audiences respond to personality. Most agencies know this and do nothing about it.

Build first, present with results.

I didn't pitch the system before building it. I built it, ran it, proved it worked, and then presented the results. It's much harder to say no to something that's already working.

Document for the person who comes after you.

A system that only works when you're running it isn't really a system. The real test was whether someone else could use it. That required documentation, templates, and simplification — as much design work as building it in the first place.
